### 1. 🖥️ Lambda Launches Bare Metal Instances with NVIDIA GB300 NVL72

Lambda launched a new bare-metal instance type that combines direct hardware access (CPU, memory, GPU, disk, TPM — no hypervisor) with a fully API-driven lifecycle. Starting with the NVIDIA GB300 NVL72 rack-scale system, Bare Metal Instances use NVIDIA BlueField DPUs in zero-trust mode for provisioning and BMCs for out-of-band health monitoring. This bridges the gap between raw bare-metal control and cloud-grade programmability — a major play in the BMaaS space.

**Source:** https://lambda.ai/blog/lambda-bare-metal-instances

### 2. 🚀 Senao SX906: Intel Xeon 6 SoC DPU on a PCIe Card (Computex 2026)

Senao showed a beast of a SmartNIC/DPU at Computex 2026 powered by Intel's Xeon 6 SoC (Granite Rapids-D). This is no low-power DPU — it starts at 24 Intel P-cores and scales to 38 cores, with up to 200Gbps network throughput, dual QSFP28 ports, 4 DDR5 channels, and an ASPEED AST2600 BMC onboard. The card consumes 295-355W and is a literal "server on a card" with 24 PCIe Gen5 lanes exposed via MCIO connectors. This could reshape how bare-metal nodes handle network offload and virtualization.

**Source:** https://www.servethehome.com/this-is-an-intel-xeon-6-soc-dpu-on-a-pcie-card-from-senao-at-computex-2026/

### 4. 🔌 Lambda Unboxes NVIDIA's First Co-Packaged Optics Switch

Lambda got early access to NVIDIA's Quantum-X Photonics Q3450-LD switch with co-packaged optics (CPO). The CPO switch draws 3.95 kW vs 7.0 kW for a standard switch — saving 3.05 kW per unit. In a 41,472-GPU cluster, that's 4.4 MW of power freed up, enough to power an equivalent of 3,137 additional GPUs. CPO also eliminates ~655K discrete transceiver modules from a large fabric, massively reducing failure points. This is the future of AI networking infrastructure.

**Source:** https://lambda.ai/blog/unbox-one-of-nvidias-first-co-packaged-optics-samples-with-lambda

### 5. 🧬 AMD openSIL + Coreboot Successfully Runs on EPYC Servers — AGESA Replaced

In a landmark achievement for open-source firmware, Phoronix confirmed AMD's openSIL (Open-Source Silicon Initialization Library) combined with Coreboot is now booting on AMD EPYC server processors. This replaces proprietary AGESA firmware with fully auditable, open-source initialization code — a major win for transparency, security, and customization in bare-metal server infrastructure. For anyone running AMD-based bare-metal, this means greater control over the firmware stack.

**Source:** https://www.phoronix.com/review/amd-opensil-coreboot-ar1

## 📊 Bare Metal Trend Summary

- **BMaaS is maturing fast** — Lambda's API-driven bare-metal instances with GB300 NVL72 show the market is converging on a model where bare-metal control meets cloud-grade API lifecycle management. The "no hypervisor, all API" approach is the new standard for AI infrastructure.

- **Network is the new bottleneck** — From CPO switches cutting 45% network power to Senao's server-grade DPU cards, the networking layer is undergoing a hardware revolution. Bare-metal operators should be watching CPO adoption and DPU offload strategies closely.

- **Open-source firmware gains server traction** — AMD's openSIL + Coreboot success on EPYC is a watershed moment for firmware transparency. This opens the door for custom firmware in bare-metal deployments — critical for security-conscious enterprises.

- **RISC-V inches toward enterprise readiness** — Red Hat's continued RHEL support for RISC-V signals that the architecture is no longer just academic. Bare-metal edge and specialized workloads will be the first to adopt.

- **AI infrastructure funding is unrelenting** — Lambda's $1B facility (nearly 4x its previous round) shows the capital velocity behind dedicated AI hardware. The market is voting with dollars: AI workloads run on bare metal, not shared virtualized infrastructure.
